Bypassing YouTube's video checking system

A small research on YouTube's content detection.

This entry has the objective of showcasing the result of an investigation about YouTube's checking feauture, specifically, how does the platform tackle the detection of content which goes against Terms of Service (ToS). The main reason behind this research is to see how AI, paired with other detection techniques, hold up against certain tricks.

It is of upmost importance to note everything explained here has been performed under curiousity viewpoints, by no means does this have the intention to be replicated.

I. What is YouTube? +

YouTube is an online video sharing platform founded in February of 2005 and purchased by Google in 2006. While it initially only served as a space to upload videos, as the years passed it began to expand to other services like live streaming, music (YouTube Music), broadcasting (YouTubeTV) and short content (YouTube Shorts). As of 2026, it is the biggest video platform in the world, having 20 million uploads a day.

II. Video upload process +

Logically, uploading a video on the platform is not instant. In fact, the video undergoes a process that can be divided into four distinct phases: Details, video elements, checks and visibility. All of this takes place in the portal of creation known as YouTube Studio.



After taking a closer look at each one of them, the following key points can be extracted from each phase:

Details
In this section the major configuration of the content takes place; Title, description, thumbnail, playlist, COPPA...
Video elements
As the name suggests, YouTube provides the option to add elements to the material such as subtitles, end screens and cards.
Checks
The main topic of the entry, visually it alerts the user of copyright infringements or ToS issues, the inner workings are explored in point III.
Visibility
Last but not least the user decides if the material is going to be public, unlisted or private. An upload schedule can also be specified.

III. System analysis (approximation) +

After understanding the process behind an upload from the frontend, it is time to analyze the backend. As expected, the complete details of how the checking system operates is private. However, there is some brief documentation available published by Google, check YouTube Content ID (Google), YouTube Content ID (History) and YouTube Content ID (Copyrightcheck) accordingly.

To begin the analysis it is key to understand that a video, as obvious as it is, can contain images and sound. Basing off that premise, it can be deduced the utilized software is capable of distinguishing between both sources. This essentially provides the following conclusion: There are two systems. While there is no viable way of observing internal operations, the working pipeline can be replicated by researching fingerprinting technology.

SYSTEM A: SOUND
The sound system will be unpacked first since it is not the core of the entry. How does a computer compare two audios and identify they are equal?

The answer is found in the audio's time-frequency representation (Spectrogram || Mel-Spectrogram) which can be analyzed to spot frequency peaks, rhythmic patterns, harmonic relationship and temportal structure. As one might expect, the representation is obtained by complex mathematical formulas, I have provided two of them to satisfy curiosity: An approximate representation of a spectogram and spectral peaks:




According to sources, YouTube licensed technology from a company called AudibleMagic in the mid-2000s. So after using said software, they most likely uploaded all that data into a database to achieve said comparations. Fortunately, some people have made tools with similar functionalities: Chromaprint by AcousticID, Dejavu by WorldVeil or Panako by JorenSix.

Furthermore, big systems rely on vectors rather than "handcrafted hashes" to perform what is known as a Nearest neighbor search with resources such as FAISS, which practically integrate AI solutions to reduce computational costs.

BYPASSING THE SOUND SYSTEM
While these systems are pretty well developed, the system can be easily bypassed if one focuses on what it is being analyzed. For instance, if someone was to modify the pitch and velocity and paired it up with an equalizer it would most likely not be detected.

SYSTEM B: VIDEO
Back when I first started stress-testing the system in 2020, I noticed that the company had no way of checking the millions of videos on a frame to frame scale. This fact is important because to ensure a proper check, the system would have take chunks of n frames every n seconds and on top of those obtained frames, execute the corresponding algorithms. This is known as keyframe extraction which is paired with perceptual hashing algorithms such as aHash, dHash and pHash. Another major technique to note is the inclusion of local invariant descriptors (SIFT || SURF) that manage to identify imagery even if it's scaled, rotated or cropped.

BYPASSING THE VIDEO SYSTEM
The same problem emerges, as sophisticated as these systems are, if one knows how to manipulate the display of the forbidden material taking into account the functionality of algorithms, the system will not detect it. In this case, I present multiple different techniques that accomplish just that.



This approximation is important to understand the methodology of points IV, V and VI so please refer to the different documentation links provided while you proceed reading through this entry. Time to turn theory into practice!

IV. Frame Injection +

NOTE: In order to exemplify the different techiques from this point onwards easily, consider the following: White is the allowed content, grey is the forbidden content.

Frame Injection, as the name implies, is a simple technique which consists of introducing the content we want to hide in a single frame. The rest of the video will contain normal clips of way greater duration to decrease the possibilities of said frame getting caught. It is the stealthiest and most simple method to hide content, although it does require the most user interaction, that being setting the playback speed to x0.5 or x0.25 in order to visualize the content on the platform. V. Filter Hiding +

Filter Hiding is a more hands-on approach on the content we want to hide. While Frame Injection relies on having a complementary content, Filter Hiding does not, making the need of user interaction deprecated. In essence, the media is to be protected by the addition of the most impactful filters your video editor provides such as "Invert colours", "Glitch" or "Distortion". Naturally, the less filters are used, the cleaner and more comfortable is to watch, however, you also increase the risk of getting caught. Balance is what makes Filter Hiding stand out. VI. Image on image +

Image on image is the riskiest yet clearest way to publish forbidden media on the platform. The name is explains it, on the video editor use the allowed video in a layer above the imaginery to hide. Then, play around with the opacity of the normal media to make the other content see-through. After playing around with some values on different channels, the threshold I was able to conclude is 80%~85% opacity, going any lower or higher would lead to either getting the channel flagged or make the forbidden media way too hard to notice.
